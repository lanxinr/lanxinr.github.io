1. Scenario Description

In an undergraduate academic writing course, the instructor works with students who have completed basic instruction in academic writing and can produce organized drafts, but who remain uncertain about how to revise effectively. While most students understand that revision is expected, they often treat it as surface correction‚Äîfixing grammar or wording‚Äîrather than as deliberate work on paragraph focus, sentence-to-sentence logic, or clarity of meaning for an academic reader.

Across drafts, several patterns recur. Paragraphs frequently lack a clear controlling idea, with topic sentences that do not clearly signal what the paragraph will develop. Sentences may be individually grammatical but loosely connected, leaving readers to infer relationships that should be explicit. Claims are sometimes stated clearly but not fully supported or explained, resulting in gaps between ideas and evidence. These underlying issues are often obscured by language-level problems such as long, tangled sentences or repetitive phrasing.

Like many instructors teaching writing, this instructor has limited capacity to provide timely, repeated, and individualized feedback on every draft, particularly at the sentence and paragraph level across multiple revision cycles. To address this constraint while maintaining student responsibility for writing, the instructor integrates a recurring writing practice that occurs approximately every two weeks within a 60-minute class session. An AI-based feedback tool is incorporated deliberately as support for revision and evaluation, not for content generation. The instructor‚Äôs primary concern is not whether students find AI helpful, but whether they can explain why specific revision decisions improve focus, logic, or clarity in ways that can be evaluated across drafts over multiple cycles.

2. Learning Objectives

Across repeated cycles of this practice, students are expected to improve their ability to:

  • Revise paragraphs so that each one has a clear topic sentence and supporting details that directly develop that focus, using AI-based feedback tools (e.g., ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) selectively to identify and address weaknesses in focus through revision, rather than to generate content.
  • Improve sentence-to-sentence clarity and logical flow by evaluating AI feedback about wording, connections, or emphasis, and demonstrating through revision which suggestions improve readability for an academic reader.
  • Increase clarity of meaning by revising sentences that are vague, overloaded, or ambiguous, while maintaining the writer‚Äôs intended claim and stance, as evidenced by consistency across revised passages.
  • Explain revision decisions by articulating why specific AI-supported or independent changes improved paragraph focus, sentence-level clarity, or alignment between claims and supporting details, as reflected in differences between original and revised drafts, rather than accepting AI feedback automatically.

Progress toward these objectives is observed across drafts and explanations of revision choices, reflecting developing judgment in both writing and responsible AI use.

3. Activity Process

Phase 1: Independent Drafting (‚âà10‚Äì15 minutes)

Students work with a complete draft of a short academic text written independently. In some cycles, students draft during class; in others, they arrive with a draft prepared in advance. In both cases, the emphasis remains on independent drafting before AI use. The draft reflects the student‚Äôs own ideas and argument and is not expected to be polished. Many drafts show promising thinking alongside unclear paragraph focus or weak sentence connections. The instructor treats the draft as evidence of thinking rather than a finished product.

Students briefly review their own draft and identify specific areas of concern, such as a paragraph whose main point is unclear or a sequence of sentences that does not read smoothly. This self-identification anchors later feedback.

Phase 2: Focused Use of AI for Feedback (‚âà10‚Äì15 minutes)

Students turn to the AI tools with questions about specific sentences or short passages they have already identified. In practice, some students initially attempt broad requests. When this happens, the instructor redirects them briefly and concretely.

A typical contrast at this stage is:

  • Unfocused request: ‚ÄúRewrite this paragraph to sound more academic.‚Äù
  • Focused question: ‚ÄúDoes this topic sentence clearly signal what the paragraph is about, or is the problem in how the next sentence connects to it?‚Äù

Through repeated redirection, students learn to use AI as a reader that highlights possible problems in focus, sentence connections, or wording. Learning to identify where focus, logic, or clarity breaks down is itself a core learning goal, and early-stage misidentification is treated as a normal and productive part of the process, supported through brief instructor guidance. AI responses sometimes clarify where meaning breaks down and sometimes offer alternatives that oversimplify or subtly shift emphasis. The instructor reinforces that AI feedback is informational, not authoritative.

Phase 3: Student-Directed Revision (‚âà15‚Äì20 minutes)

Students revise their drafts manually, deciding which suggestions to accept, adapt, or reject. The final authority for all revision decisions remains with the student, not the AI tools. At first, many students apply suggestions broadly. Over repeated cycles, a common shift occurs: students begin to keep revisions that sharpen paragraph focus or clarify logical links, while rejecting changes that weaken their claim or flatten their voice.

This phase makes learning visible. The instructor observes not only changes in the text, but changes in how students talk about those changes‚Äîmoving from ‚Äúthe AI said this was better‚Äù to ‚Äúthis makes the connection between my claim and example clearer.‚Äù

Phase 4: Reflection and Instructor Feedback (‚âà10‚Äì15 minutes)

Students submit their revised draft with brief notes explaining key revision decisions. The reflection focuses on why a change improved paragraph focus, sentence flow, or clarity of meaning, not on describing AI tools or features.

Instructor feedback builds on these explanations, often returning to concrete questions such as whether a paragraph now stays focused on its main idea, whether sentences guide the reader logically, or whether claims and supporting details are better aligned.

4. Student Artifacts

Each writing cycle produces a consistent set of artifacts:

Original Draft

The original draft represents the student‚Äôs independent thinking before any AI-supported feedback is used. It provides a baseline for examining paragraph focus, sentence-to-sentence logic, and clarity of meaning as initially expressed by the student.

Revised Draft

The revised draft shows how the student has addressed issues of paragraph organization, logical flow between sentences, and clarity through manual revision. Differences between the original and revised versions make visible which problems the student prioritized and how effectively those problems were resolved.

AI Interaction Evidence (brief excerpts or notes)

Students submit brief excerpts or notes showing where AI feedback was consulted and the type of question asked (e.g., about paragraph focus, sentence connections, or wording). This evidence is limited in scope and descriptive rather than interpretive, serving to clarify how the AI was used as a source of feedback, not to explain or justify revision decisions.

Short Reflection on Revision Decisions

Students provide a brief reflection explaining why specific revisions improved paragraph focus, sentence-to-sentence logic, or clarity of meaning, and why certain AI suggestions were accepted, adapted, or rejected. The reflection focuses on concrete revision decisions and their effects on the text, and is written in the student‚Äôs own terms, foregrounding independent judgment and authorship rather than descriptions of AI use.

5. Evaluation Criteria and Performance Levels

Criterion Strong Adequate Weak
Paragraph Focus Each paragraph has a clear topic sentence; supporting details directly develop that focus. Main idea is present but unevenly developed. Paragraph lacks a clear focus or shifts between ideas.
Sentence-to-Sentence Logic Sentences are clearly connected through explicit logical relationships (e.g., cause, contrast, elaboration). Some connections are implicit or weak. Sentences read as isolated or loosely related.
Strategic AI Use AI feedback is used selectively; the student can explain why suggestions were adopted, adapted, or rejected. AI feedback is mostly accepted with limited explanation. AI feedback is applied wholesale or without understanding.
Consistency of Meaning Revisions clarify meaning without altering the writer‚Äôs claim or stance. Minor shifts in emphasis or tone appear. Meaning or argument becomes unclear or inconsistent.
Transparency & Integrity AI use is documented clearly, and revision decisions reflect independent authorship and judgment. AI use is documented but explanations are vague or incomplete. AI use is unclear, undocumented, or obscures authorship.

6. Guidelines for Faculty

Preparation

  • Anticipate that students will equate revision with correction rather than rethinking focus or logic.
  • Clarify that AI supports evaluation and revision, not content generation.

During the Activity

  • Redirect broad ‚Äúrewrite this‚Äù requests toward questions about paragraph focus or sentence connections.
  • Listen closely to how students explain their revisions; explanations reveal more than the text alone.
  • Accept uneven judgment early on as part of the learning process.

After the Activity

  • Frame feedback around concrete writing decisions rather than general quality.
  • Address recurring issues (e.g., paragraph drift, weak sentence links) collectively.
  • Use patterns from one cycle to adjust emphasis in the next, reinforcing the iterative nature of the practice.

7. Guidelines for Students

Preparation

  • Produce a complete draft independently, with each paragraph attempting to address a single main idea.
  • Read the draft once without revising and identify specific problems, such as unclear paragraph focus, weak sentence connections, or confusing wording.

During the Activity

  • Use AI-based feedback tools primarily on sentences or short passages you have already identified as problematic.
  • Ask questions that help you evaluate focus, logic, or clarity rather than requesting rewrites.
  • Compare AI suggestions directly with your original wording and decide whether each one strengthens paragraph focus, clarifies sentence relationships, or sharpens meaning.
  • Accept, adapt, or reject AI suggestions based on their effect on argument and voice.

After the Activity

  • Revise the draft manually so that all changes reflect your own decisions.
  • Review original and revised versions side by side to identify where focus, logic, or clarity improved.
  • Write a brief revision reflection (approximately 200 words) explaining why specific revisions improved the writing and why certain AI suggestions were not used.
  • Document where AI feedback influenced the revision, so your decision-making process is transparent.
